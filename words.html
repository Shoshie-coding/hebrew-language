Common Hebrew Phrases

Hebrew Root Words

In Semitic languages, and Hebrew is no exception, most words derive from a three-consonant root word that contains the intrinsic meaning of the word. This root word is called shoresh and by adding letters at the beginning and end of the root, you get various parts of speech like nouns, verbs and adjectives. You will see further on that it is normal for Hebrew words have more than one meaning. This is also because modern Hebrew gave old words new meanings to make up for the lack of modern words.

We hope that you enjoy the below selection of Hebrew words and that you learn something new about this beautiful language!

Shalom (שלום)

Shalom is perhaps the most well-known Hebrew word. It means hello, goodbye, and peace. It is also found in the saying Shalom Aleichem (Hebrew: שָׁלוֹם עֲלֵיכֶם‎, 'Peace be upon you') which is also a traditional song sung by Jews every Friday night upon returning home from synagogue prayer on the eve of the Shabbat.

Ken (כן) and Lo (לֹא)

Ken means Yes and Lo means No - we can see this in the famous brand Kenvelo – which literally means Yes and No (ve means 'and').

Slicha (סליחה)

Slicha means sorry or excuse me.

Toda (תודה)

Toda means thank you it’s used often in the phrase Toda raba meaning thank you very much.

BeVakasha (בבקשה)

In Hebrew BeVakasha is used as a way of saying 'with pleasure' or 'you're welcome' and 'please'. When someone says Toda 'Thank you', you can reply with BeVakasha. Similarly, you can ask something from someone using BeVakasha in the sentence.

Mayim (מים)

Mayim means 'water' – the interesting fact about Mayim is that’s it’s a dual noun. Dual nouns in Hebrew usually have no singular form.

Shamayim (שמיים)

A similar dual noun is Shamayim which means 'heavens' or 'skies'.

Mazel Tov (מזל טוב)

Mazel Tov literally means 'good luck' and 'congratulations'. It's something people say to someone getting married.

Ma Shlomcha? (מה שלומך)

This is a way of asking someone 'How are you?', it literally means 'what (is) your peace?'. You probably notice the word Shalom in Shlomcha because there are from the same Hebrew root Shalem (שלם) that means 'whole' and 'complete'. Similarly, Ma Nishma (מה נשמע) means 'What's up?'.

Baruch HaShem (ברוך השם)

It literally means 'Blessed be the name' where name refers to God, in Judaism you're not supposed to pronounce God's name as his name is holy as God is holy. It's very versatile and can mean different things depending on the context, for example it's used to say, 'I can't complain' or 'It is what it is' to say you accept a certain situation.

Kol HaKavod (כל הכבוד)

'Good job' or 'well done' (lit: all the respect).

Beseder (בסדר)

Beseder is used in many phrases to express that something or someone is OK. For example, HaKol Beseder (הכול בסדר) means 'everything is OK', whereas Yihye BeSeder (יהיה בסדר) means 'It will be OK' or 'no worries'.

Chaval al Ha’Zman (חבל על הזמן)

It literally means 'a waste of time' but it can have various meanings, colloquially, it means 'forget it' and in slang it can also be used as an expletive 'great' or 'wonderful'.

Nu (נו)

Nu is a two-letter word that comes from Yiddish and is used colloquially to say'So?', 'c’mon already', 'go on'.

Chutzpah (חוצפה)

This slang word comes from Yiddish, and it means 'nerve', 'audacity'.

Sababa (סבבה)

This word comes from Arabic and it's a slang way of saying 'cool' or 'awesome'

Yalla (יאללה)

This slang word comes from Arabic as well and it means 'let's go', 'go ahead'.
